The General Theory of Employment, Interest, and Money


            Author(s): John Maynard Keynes
            Date Finished: 4/2024
            Rating: 7/10

A small preface to this review. This book is pretty dense and not for the faint of heart. The author also includes a disclaimer that you may not understand if you are more of a layperson. I would not recommend this book unless you are specifically on the foundation that keynes builds his ideas.

He starts out with talking about the how his main focus in this text is the general case of certain economic phenomena rather than special cases. He follows this up by talking about the 2 main types of unemployement voluntary and involuntary, respectively. Frictional unemployement is more a consequence of living in a imperfect system than the fault of any party. Some common types of frictional unemployement may include trying to find a job in a field that you recently got fired from instead of getting a job in fast food or the like. This could also take the form of wages not being extended down far enough because of collective bargaining. This is the case sometimes because businesses want to pay the minimum amount possible and at that wage they would also have more capital to hire more workers. Voluntary unemployement is a little bit different in the case that the individual is not working for a multitude of reasons. The main reason given by Keynes is that when the marginial disutility of your labor is greater than the wage you may receive. This makes it not worth your time to work at the job. Another possibility for not working could be unemployement benefits, especially if those benefits are greater than the wage that you would have otherwise received. One reason not stated that may fit is that of a disability, you may be able to work but the effort required and time spent may outweigh the wage that you are payed. His last statement in this chapter revolves around the nominal and the real wage. The nominal wage is the number amount of money you are payed, e.g. 20 dollars an hour. The real wage is a more abstract concept that refers more to the amount of goods that you can buy with the amount of money you make. For example 1 dollars an hour sounded pretty good in 1800, but nowadays with inflation and other effects that have raised the price of goods, 1 dollar an hour is very little. He also bring up that labor/labor unions really only set the nominal wage. Nowadays, we do have unions calling for "living wages" which is kind of a call to real wages, but more often you see people talking about a rise in the nominal price, e.g. 20 dollars an hour to 25.

One concept that this author stresses is the idea that marginal wages are at a maximum equal to the marginal disutility of labor. This effectively sets the maximum amount of possible employement, which may correspond to "full employement". This is the case because you can only hire so many people given a minimum and maximum wage, and a set amount of capital. He then leads into the idea of how investement affects employement which then affects consumption. Investement basically increases the amount of capital that employers can spend toward employees and cet. par. employement increases. You need money in order to buy and then consume goods which is gained from a job. This is why some amount of investement is helpful for growth of industry and jobs. One very irionic statement made by Keynes near the end of this chapter talks about how Ricardos ideas were suited to the economic climate of the time period around 1800 but fails to recognize that the success of his own ideas was, at least partially due to the economic climate that he lived in. This leads to the question of why shouldn't we just be constantly moving labor around to the most efficent activity? This is because at first a growing industry will want to pull in workers or goods that will create the the most efficent production of goods. Since there are not an infinite amount of resources that are well suited to that particular industry this will lead to less and less efficent resources that are pulled into said industry. This is easily summed up by the idea of diminishing returns.

He also mentions that just because that we are expecting a certain price for our goods does not mean that said price will come to pass. There is a time delay between starting production of a good and selling the good. If you heavily invest in a good whose price is rising and produce a lot of it, the price will decrease when the time comes to actually sell the good. This is especially true if other firms also ramp up their production of a good in relation to the price increase. He then introduces different types of costs for capital or machines. He talks about supplementary costs, which are basically repair costs, and how they affect your choice to invest in said device. He introduces the second type of cost which is windfall cost. This is comprised of unpredictable costs which are not controlled for. Logically you can't really anticipate windfall costs because they then seek to be windfall costs. While at the same time you have a pretty good estimate of how much it will cost to upkeep a unit of production when you purchase it. He again talks about investment increases employment which then increase maximum output. Saving your money in a bank is similar because you are increasing your money according to the rate of interest, and the bank is also able to loan out your money to other people. Investment will not happen if the investor has nothing to gain from the investment and if the investee has to make the investment worth it or the investor would choose somewhere else to invest. At the same time if everyone invests and no one saves that would probably lead to a collapse of the banks because at least some of the loans would be defaulted on. If the banks have no money because no one is saving then they are unable to pay back on defaulted loans.
